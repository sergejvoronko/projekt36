The E36's rear suspension is a brilliant multi-link design, but its handling characteristics are critically dependent on the health of one specific component: the Rear Trailing Arm Bushing, or RTAB. This large bushing is the main pivot point for the entire rear trailing arm. When it fails, it introduces unwanted dynamic toe changes, transforming a precise-handling machine into an unpredictable mess.

After 25+ years, your original E36 RTABs are not just worn; they are expired. Replacement isn't a question of *if*, but *when* and *with what*. Your choice between OEM rubber, polyurethane, and spherical bearings will fundamentally define your car's rear-end behavior. This guide breaks down the options, costs, and real-world implications to help you make the right decision for your build.

## The Problem: Why Factory RTABs Fail

The RTAB's job is to allow the trailing arm to pivot up and down with suspension travel while resisting lateral (side-to-side) and longitudinal (fore-aft) forces. The stock BMW E36 RTAB (Part No. `33326770824`) is a soft rubber bushing with large voids designed to absorb Noise, Vibration, and Harshness (NVH).

This comfort-oriented design is the component's Achilles' heel. Under hard cornering, braking, and acceleration, the soft rubber deflects, allowing the rear wheel to change its toe angle dynamically. A new OEM bushing is a compromise; a worn one is a liability.

**Symptoms of failed RTABs include:**
*   **Rear-end "steering":** The car feels like it's trying to steer from the back, especially during weight transfer (e.g., hard acceleration or cornering).
*   **Instability under braking:** The rear of the car may feel twitchy or wander when you get on the brakes.
*   **Clunking noises:** A metallic or rubbery "clunk" from the rear when going over bumps or during acceleration.
*   **Uneven rear tire wear:** Significant inner-edge tire wear is a classic sign of worn bushings allowing excessive toe-out.

On a 25-year-old E36, even one with low mileage, the original rubber has hardened and perished. It no longer has the elasticity to do its job, leading to excessive play and the symptoms above.

## Option 1: OEM Rubber – The Factory Baseline & OEM+

For a daily driver, restoration project, or a car where comfort is paramount, staying with a rubber bushing is a valid choice. However, not all OEM rubber is created equal.

The standard E36 RTAB is soft and contains voids to improve compliance. A far better choice, and one of the most popular OEM+ upgrades in the E36 community, is to use the RTAB from the E46 M3 or Z4M.

| Part Description | Part Number | Approx. EU Price (Pair) | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|---|
| Standard E36 RTAB (Lemförder) | `33 32 6 770 824` | €40 - €55 | Soft, voided rubber for maximum comfort. |
| **E46 M3 / Z4M RTAB (Lemförder)** | `33 32 6 770 817` | €50 - €70 | Solid rubber, ~30% stiffer than stock E36. |

The E46 M3 RTAB is a direct fit and is made from a solid block of higher-durometer rubber. This simple change significantly reduces unwanted deflection without a meaningful penalty in NVH. It's the perfect solution for the enthusiast who wants to sharpen their car's reflexes while maintaining its civility for daily use.

In our experience, the difference is immediately noticeable. The rear end feels more connected and predictable, especially when applying power out of a corner. For the cost, it's one of the best value-for-money upgrades you can perform on an E36's suspension.

**Verdict:**
*   **Best for:** Daily drivers, restorations, OEM+ builds.
*   **Choose the E46 M3/Z4M bushing (`33326770817`).** There is no logical reason to install the softer, voided E36 part when this superior OEM option exists for a negligible price difference.

## Option 2: Polyurethane – The Street/Track Compromise

Polyurethane (poly) is the go-to choice for most E36 enthusiasts building a dual-purpose street and track car. Poly bushings replace the compliant rubber with a much stiffer, more durable polymer, drastically reducing deflection and providing a more direct connection between the chassis and the road.

Polyurethane bushings are typically sold with a durometer rating (e.g., 80A, 95A, measured on the Shore A scale), which indicates their hardness.

*   **75A-85A (Street):** A significant step up from rubber. Offers a great balance of improved performance and manageable NVH for a spirited daily driver.
*   **90A-95A (Track/Race):** Very stiff. Provides precision nearing that of a spherical bearing, but with a corresponding increase in noise and vibration transmitted into the cabin.

Leading brands like Powerflex and Strongflex offer excellent, well-engineered kits.

For our Project 328i track car, we installed 95A Powerflex Black Series RTABs. The change was transformative. The rear of the car is now completely stable under threshold braking, and the "squirm" on corner exit is gone. The trade-off is a palpable increase in road and differential noise inside the car, and sharp bumps are felt more acutely.

**Crucial Installation Note:** Polyurethane bushings MUST be installed with the supplied grease (typically a copper or silicone-based lubricant). Apply it liberally to all contact surfaces—the inner sleeve, the outer surface of the bushing, and the trailing arm bore. Failure to do so will result in suspension binding and persistent, maddening squeaking.

**Verdict:**
*   **Best for:** Most enthusiasts. Dual-purpose street/track cars.
*   **Recommendation:** Start with an 80A/85A bushing. Only step up to 95A if your car spends more time on the track than the street and you can tolerate the NVH penalty.

## Option 3: Spherical Bearings – The Uncompromising Race Solution

For a dedicated race, drift, or time-attack E36, there is no substitute for a spherical bearing. This option completely removes any compliant material (rubber or poly) and replaces it with a precision-machined housing containing a high-load spherical bearing (similar to a massive heim joint).

The result is zero deflection. The trailing arm pivots with near-zero friction and absolutely no unwanted movement. This provides the driver with the ultimate in feedback, precision, and control over the rear axle. The geometry remains precisely where the alignment says it should be, regardless of the loads involved.

This performance comes at a significant cost, both in Euros and in comfort.

**The Reality of Spherical Bearings:**
Spherical bearings transmit every single vibration, noise, and impact from the road surface directly into the chassis. There is zero isolation. The ride is harsh, and differential whine becomes a prominent interior soundtrack. They are also a wear item that requires more frequent inspection than a bushing. For these reasons, they are entirely unsuitable for a car that sees regular street driving.

**Verdict:**
*   **Best for:** Dedicated, no-compromise race cars.
*   **Recommendation:** Only choose this option if your E36 is trailered to the track and performance is the only metric that matters.

## Installation: The Right Tool for the Job

Regardless of which bushing you choose, the removal and installation process is the same. The RTAB is pressed into the trailing arm with significant interference fit. Attempting to remove it with a hammer and chisel or a generic puller set will likely end in frustration and a damaged trailing arm.

A dedicated E36/E46 RTAB puller/installer tool is not optional; it is essential.

| Tool Type | Example Part Number | Approx. EU Price |
|---|---|---|
| E36/E46 RTAB Tool | BGS Technic `B1126` | €80 - €120 |

These tools use a long threaded rod and precisely-shaped cups to press the old bushing out and pull the new one in straight and without damage. The investment in the right tool will save you hours of labor and potential damage.

The basic process involves unbolting the RTAB carrier bracket from the chassis, which allows the trailing arm to swing down slightly. The tool is then used to extract the old bushing and install the new one directly on the car. Polyurethane bushings are often two-piece and can be installed by hand after the outer metal sleeve of the original bushing has been removed.

**Crucially, a full four-wheel alignment is mandatory after replacing the RTABs.** The new bushings will have altered the static toe of the rear wheels.

## What's Next?

Choosing the right RTAB is a foundational step in building a well-sorted E36. For 90% of owners, the choice between the E46 M3 rubber bushing and a street-focused polyurethane bushing will deliver the desired results. The former restores OEM+ precision with no downsides, while the latter trades a little comfort for a significant leap in performance. Spherical bearings remain the exclusive domain of the serious racer.

With your RTABs addressed, the rear end is now predictable and stable. The next logical upgrades to create a truly locked-down rear subframe assembly are stiffer subframe and differential bushings. By systematically replacing the E36's aging rubber, you unlock the full potential of this legendary chassis.
