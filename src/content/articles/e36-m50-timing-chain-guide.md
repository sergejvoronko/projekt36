## How the M50 Timing Chain System Works

The M50 — whether you're running the non-VANOS M50B20/B25 or the VANOS-equipped M50B20TU/B25TU — uses a single-row roller chain to drive both camshafts off the crankshaft. The chain runs up the front of the block and passes through a tensioner rail on the slack side, a fixed guide rail on the drive side, and an upper guide bridge that spans both cams at the top of the chain run.

The tensioner itself is a hydraulic unit fed by engine oil pressure. It pushes against the tensioner rail (the long curved plastic-backed guide on the left side of the chain when viewed from the front) to maintain chain tension. When oil pressure drops — cold starts, low oil level, worn pump — the tensioner can momentarily lose pressure, allowing the chain to slap. Over time, this hammers the guide rails into fragments.

On the VANOS variants (TU engines), there's an additional complication: the VANOS unit sits at the top of the chain drive and adds its own oil pressure demand. A failing VANOS solenoid or worn seals can indirectly starve the chain tensioner at idle.

## Known Failure Modes and Symptoms

This is the most important section. The M50 doesn't usually give you a lot of warning before timing chain problems become catastrophic.

**Tensioner failure** is the most common root cause. The hydraulic tensioner — BMW part **11 31 1 703 430** (superseded to **11 31 1 748 052** on later service parts) — uses a one-way valve and a small oil reservoir to maintain pressure between starts. When the check valve wears or the piston seal degrades, oil bleeds back overnight. The next cold start, you get 2–4 seconds of chain rattle before pressure builds. Most owners dismiss this as "cold start noise." It isn't. Every one of those rattles is the chain slapping the plastic guide rails.

**Guide rail cracking** follows. The upper guide bridge (**11 31 1 703 428**) and the tensioner rail (**11 31 1 703 429**) are glass-fibre reinforced plastic. On a 25-year-old E36, the plastic has been heat-cycled thousands of times. Chunks break off, drop into the oil pan, and can block the oil pickup. At that point you're not just doing a timing job — you're pulling the pan and potentially replacing the oil pump.

**Symptoms to watch for:**

| Symptom | Likely Cause |
|---|---|
| Rattling on cold start, clears in <5 sec | Tensioner bleed-down, early stage |
| Rattle persists past 10 seconds | Tensioner failure or severely worn rail |
| Rattle at idle when warm | Rails disintegrating, chain has excessive slack |
| Rough idle + timing rattle | VANOS seals gone on TU engines |
| Metal debris in oil pan | Guide rail fragments — drop the pan now |
| Ticking from cam cover area | Upper guide bridge cracked or displaced |

If you're buying an M50-powered E36 with unknown history and more than 180,000 km on the clock, budget for this job before you drive it hard.

## Parts: OEM, OEM-Supplier, and What to Avoid

For a complete M50 timing chain refresh, you need to address every wear item in the system. Replacing only the tensioner and leaving 200,000 km rails in place is a waste of time.

| Timing cover gasket | 11 14 1 705 406 | Elring | €12–€18 |

**Total OEM-quality kit: approximately €230–€330 sourced through Autodoc, TRW, or a German BMW specialist like Rial or TF-Sportscars.**

**What to avoid:** Generic Chinese-sourced timing kits marketed under no-name brands on eBay. The chain pitch and roller diameter tolerances are often outside spec, which means uneven wear on your sprockets within 30,000 km. INA and Iwis supply BMW directly — there is no reason to use anything else for the chain itself. Febi and Elring are acceptable for guide rails and gaskets.

**For VANOS engines (TU variants):** While the cover is off, replace the VANOS sealing rings (**11 36 1 440 134**, Teflon ring set, ~€15) and the VANOS solenoid filter if it hasn't been done (**11 36 1 740 969**, ~€8). These do not require the VANOS unit to be removed if you're already in there.

## The Job: Key Steps and Where People Go Wrong

This isn't a step-by-step torque wrench guide — you have the Bentley or a TIS printout for that. What follows are the decisions and details that trip people up.

**Set TDC properly before anything else.** BMW's cam locking tool is **11 3 240** (bar across both cam lobes at TDC). A fabricated equivalent works. Do not rely on the timing marks alone — they're reference points, not precise enough for a chain swap without secondary confirmation.

**Don't remove the VANOS unit unless it's faulty.** On TU engines, the VANOS can stay in place for a chain swap if you're careful. Removing it adds 2 hours and requires the Teflon ring replacement mentioned above. If oil pressure at the VANOS is suspect, pull it. If not, leave it.

**The tensioner comes out with the cover on.** The hydraulic tensioner unscrews from the front cover (19mm hex plug) without removing the whole cover. If you're only replacing the tensioner on an otherwise low-km engine, this is a 30-minute job. But if you're already doing guides and chain, pull the cover for full access.

**Front cover alignment is where engines get ruined.** The front cover carries the front crank seal and aligns to the block with dowel pins. If you reinstall it misaligned and torque it down, you crack the cover or shim the seal off-axis. Clean the mating faces, use new gaskets, and confirm dowel engagement before touching the fasteners.

**In our M50 swap build**, we pulled a B25TU with 210,000 km before dropping it into a project E30. The tensioner check valve was completely dead — zero resistance when tested — and the tensioner rail had a 60mm chunk missing from the bottom third. The fragment was sitting in the oil pan sump. We were lucky it hadn't blocked the pickup. Full kit with VANOS seals and a new front crank seal came to €268 via Autodoc DE.

## Torque Specs and Critical Fasteners

| Fastener | Torque |
|---|---|
| Camshaft sprocket bolts | 105 Nm |
| Crankshaft sprocket centre bolt | 410 Nm (impact gun, or hold flywheel) |
| Front cover bolts (M6) | 10 Nm |
| Front cover bolts (M8) | 22 Nm |
| Tensioner plug (19mm hex) | 30 Nm |
| Cam cover bolts | 10 Nm |

The crank bolt is always the discussion point. BMW spec is 410 Nm — this is not achievable with a bar and breaker in most configurations. An impact wrench set to maximum is the practical solution. The bolt is a stretch bolt on some variants; check yours before reuse. If there's any doubt, replace it (**11 21 1 717 471**, ~€6).

## What's Next

A timing chain job on the M50 is the natural companion to front cover work and VANOS service. If the engine is out of the car for a swap — as it often is in an M50-into-E30 or E36 project — this is also the moment to address the oil pump (**11 41 1 705 198**, ~€55 for OEM-quality Hella unit), the water pump, and the thermostat housing if any of those are original.

Once timing is sorted and confirmed, the M50 is a fundamentally robust engine. They run comfortably to 300,000+ km with clean oil and a chain system that's been properly maintained. The failure mode covered here is almost entirely preventable — it's a maintenance item that got ignored, not a design flaw that can't be solved.
